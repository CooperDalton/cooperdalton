---
title: Building Tiny Systems That Compound
date: 2026-03-12
summary: A note on designing workflows that get more useful every week instead of more complicated.
---

Most good systems start small enough to feel almost too simple.

That is usually the point. A tiny system is easy to trust. You can run it every day without needing a reset, a fresh notebook, or a dramatic burst of motivation. It might just be a checklist, a folder, a script, or a ritual that closes a loop you were leaving open before.

The problem is that a lot of people design systems like they are already operating at scale. They build for a future version of themselves instead of the current one. The result is friction up front and almost no payoff.

The systems that compound have a few common traits:

- They are faster to use than to avoid.
- They leave behind something reusable.
- They make the next repetition slightly easier.
- They are boring in a good way.

That second point matters most. If a workflow creates an asset every time you run it, the value stacks. Notes become documentation. documentation becomes prompts. prompts become automations. automations become leverage.

You do not need a perfect operating system for your life. You need a few reliable loops that create value faster than they create maintenance.

When I notice a process getting heavier every week, I usually take that as a sign the system is performative rather than useful. A good reset question is: what artifact should this produce, and will I be glad it exists a month from now?

If the answer is no, the workflow probably needs to shrink.
